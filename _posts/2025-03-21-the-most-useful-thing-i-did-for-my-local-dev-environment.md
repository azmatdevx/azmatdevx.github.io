For a long time I treated my local development setup like a future optimization project. I kept a mental list of improvements I wanted to make, but most of them were abstract: better automation, cleaner shell config, a more elegant workflow.

The setup started improving only when I switched from "make it impressive" to "remove one repeated annoyance at a time."

## What actually helped

The highest-value changes were small:

- faster project navigation
- fewer manual steps to start common services
- more predictable environment naming
- defaults that failed loudly instead of silently

None of those changes would make for an exciting workstation screenshot. Together, they saved attention every day.

## The pattern I now follow

If a task feels slightly irritating and I do it several times a week, it qualifies for improvement. That rule is much better than waiting for a mythical full-system refactor of my tooling.

Local environments shape the emotional texture of engineering work more than we admit. When the setup is slightly chaotic, every task begins with drag. When the setup is calm, the work starts closer to the actual problem.

That is the version of productivity advice I trust now: less optimization theater, more friction removal.
