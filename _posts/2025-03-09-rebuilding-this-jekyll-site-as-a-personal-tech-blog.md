When I first set this site up, the theme was visually solid but semantically wrong. It was built around teams, multiple authors, lifestyle categories, and generic editorial copy. None of that matched what I wanted the site to be.

So the rewrite was less about redesign and more about removing false signals.

## What I changed first

I started with the homepage and site settings:

- team language became first-person language
- author showcases disappeared
- the navigation narrowed down to posts, topics, and about
- stock placeholder sections stopped competing with the main writing

That alone changed the feel of the site more than any CSS tweak would have.

## The bigger fix

The real problem was content. A personal tech blog cannot feel personal if the newest posts are generic placeholders. I ended up replacing the stock catalog with writing that reflected actual technical interests: performance work, debugging, delivery habits, and lessons from side projects.

## Why it was worth doing

Themes often ship with opinions embedded in the structure, not just the styling. If you leave those defaults intact, the site keeps telling visitors what kind of publication it thinks it is. For a personal site, that mismatch matters.

I would rather have a simpler site that is accurately voiced than a richer one that reads like it belongs to someone else.
